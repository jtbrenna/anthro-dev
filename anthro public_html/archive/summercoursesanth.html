SUMMER 2003 COURSE OFFERINGS

 

Anth 21 - Human Cultures (online) - Professor David Houston - online - May 27-June 27. "This is an introduction to human cultures. Who we are and how we are is a vital part of understanding the world around us. This course will depend on you, the student, to actively participate and develop the themes and ideas we'll cover. This is a course that requires active engagement!"

Anth 21 - Human Cultures - Professor Robert Gordon - Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday - 9:00-11:00 a.m. - July 7-August 6. "Introduction to cultural anthropology focusing on the life ways of non-Western societies and how anthropologists study them."

Anth 23 - Anthropology of Third World Development - Professor Charles deBurlo - Monday, Wednesday, Friday - 6:00-8:30 p.m. - July 7-August 8. "A survey of the role of applied anthropology in the understanding and analysis of development efforts to alleviate (mostly) third world problems." Guest speakers and films.

Anth 26 - Physical Anthropology - Professor Catherine Holly - Monday, Wednesday, Friday - 10:00 a.m.-12:45 p.m. - May 28-June 27. "This introductory level course focuses on what it means to be human from a biocultural evolutionary perspective. The importance of such topics as evolution, adaptation and culture in the development of our species can provide useful information about why humans behave the way they do. In addition, this information can help us to understand why a medium-sized, bipedal primate came to occupy a position of dominance over many other forms of life on earth." In taking this course, students will further sharpen their critical thinking skills as well as examine life's diversity with greater appreciation and understanding.

Anth 95 - Business Anthropology (online) - Professor Jennifer Dickinson - online - May 27-June 27. "This online course combines practical and academic perspectives in the field of business anthropology, or the cross-cultural study of business values and practices. Readings and discussions will balance case studies in business cultures around the world with materials on cross-cultural marketing, responsible business practices in the world market, and issues of globalization."

Anth 95 - Storytelling Across Cultures - Professor Jennifer Dickinson - Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday - 2:00-4:00 p.m. - May 27-June 26. "This course focuses on the social and linguistic importance of stories, storytelling and jokes in different cultures. Topics include story structure and storytelling performances, as well as folktales, urban legends, gossip, myths, jokes, and 'everyday stories.'"

Anth 95 - Cities in Cultural Context - Professor Clinton Nichols - Monday, Wednesday, Friday - 10:00 a.m.-12:50 p.m. - July 7-August 8. "This course provides an introduction to a century of anthropological scholarship about cities. A variety of examples from around the world will serve as the foundation to consider contemporary Burlington and surrounding towns. Topics will include community study and globalization approaches, race and gender in cities, the relationship between culture and the built environment, and the role discourse in urban development. The course format is lecture and discussion based on readings and mini-projects that encourage the application of topics to the Burlington area or other Vermont towns."

Anth 195 - Nonhuman Primates: Apes & Monkeys - Professor Deborah Blom - Tuesday and Thursday - 1:00-4:45 p.m. - May 27-June 26. "Over centuries, humans have been fascinated while watching the behavior of apes and monkeys. They are so much like us, yet in many ways different. They have been studied to help us gain insight into our species, what it means to be human, and as a foundation for interpreting the origins of humans and their behavior. In addition, they are simply interesting in their own right. In this class we will discuss the behavior and appearance of nonhuman primates, in such arenas as their responses to their environments, social factors, diet, reproduction, social interactions, and ethics. Through this we will also shed more light on humans and the ways that we respond to our environments." The class will include many visuals, discussion, studies on Vermonters as primates, and lecture.

Anth 200 - Field Work in Archaeology - Professor James Petersen and Professor John Crock - Monday-Friday - 8:00 a.m.-5:00 p.m. - May 27-June 27. "Participants in this course explore the techniques of archaeological investigation and culture history in a Caribbean field situation, specifically in Anguilla, British West Indies, and receive an introduction to laboratory analysis of data." Background information is also presented in the classroom at UVM during the first week. This is a 6-credit course.

Anth 295/ENVS 295 - Field Methods in Environmental Research - Professor Luis Vivanco and Professor Richard Paradis - Monday, Wednesday, Friday - 9:00 a.m.-12:00 noon on Monday and Wednesday and 9:00 a.m.-4:00 p.m. on Friday - June 9-June 27. "This course examines the theoretical, practical and ethical issues in undertaking both qualitative social science and quantitative ecological research. Participants will explore different styles and techniques of management-sensitive research applying their knowledge to the Mount Mansfield area, generating and collecting data on the human uses, attitudes and impacts on this important alpine ecosystem." Students may have further opportunity to participate in this ongoing summer project as service-learning research interns. Friday classes will be held on Mount Mansfield.

Anth 295/VS 295 - GINODOKOWA ALNOBAIWI: Teaching the Abenaki Way - Professor Fred Wiseman - Monday-Friday - 8:30 a.m.-4:30 p.m. - July 14-July 25. "This course gives teachers, museum personnel and other professionals an interdisciplinary overview of Abenaki prehistory and history, spirituality, art and politics and current issues. This academic baseline will be supplemented in lecture by current issues such as reburial and recognition. We also address pedagogical issues ranging from stereotypes to art instruction to service learning. Students will also become acquainted with print, audio and video resources; people, places and organizations such as much the Abenaki Tribal Museum, and craftspeople such as a canoe builder, basket maker and story teller. To present a balanced view, we will invite a speaker from the Vermont Attorney General's office, to state Vermont's official case that Vermont should not recognize the legitimacy of the Abenaki community." Students will be evaluated by attendance, discussion participation and final projects. This is a 6-credit course.
