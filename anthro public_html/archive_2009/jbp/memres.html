RESOLUTION IN MEMORIAM

James B. Petersen

Associate Professor and Chair

Department of Anthropology

1954-2005

Presented by John Crock

Assistant Professor of Anthropology

Director, Consulting Archaeology Program

September 12, 2005

            James B. Petersen, Chair of the Department of Anthropology, was murdered on August 13, 2005, while conducting research in the Amazon.   Without provocation, he was shot while he and colleagues were robbed at a family restaurant in Iranduba, Brazil.   At the time of his death, Jim was co-directing the Central Amazon Project, a groundbreaking archaeological study of the past societies that once thrived at the heart of the world's greatest river.  

            Jim was born in Bristol, Connecticut, in 1954.   He graduated from Plainville High School in 1972 as class president and valedictorian.   After studying at Middlebury College (1972-73) and taking time off to explore the country, Jim enrolled at UVM, majoring in anthropology.   He graduated from UVM in 1979, Phi Beta Kappa.   Soon thereafter, Jim entered the Ph.D. program in Anthropology at the University of Pittsburgh.   He earned his doctorate in less than three years, a departmental record.   His dissertation, the first ever on archaeology in Vermont, is on the Winooski site, a large, multi-period Native American village site located less than a mile from campus.  

            In 1983, after Jim finished graduate school, he moved to Maine and soon thereafter accepted a position at the University of Maine at Farmington (UMF) where he founded the UMF Archaeology Research Center.   Jim served there as associate professor and Director of the Archaeology Research Center until 1997.   During his fifteen years in Maine, Jim rewrote the prehistory of the state.   He led large scale archaeological surveys of interior rivers and lakes and directed intensive excavations of nationally significant sites.  

During his time in Maine, Jim never stopped conducting research in Vermont and also expanded his research interests well beyond New England.   He taught his first of eleven biannual UVM field schools in archaeology here in Vermont in 1983.   The field school, a month-long, hands-on course, was the hands down favorite of his students and one of his greatest legacies.   Each field school he taught produced at least one graduate who later became a professional archaeologist.   He was a profoundly influential teacher who never stopped teaching and always had time for his students young and old, currently enrolled or long since graduated.  

In 1997, Jim gave up tenure at the University of Maine at Farmington and took a pay cut to accept a full-time assistant professor position at his beloved alma mater.   Moving back to Vermont with his wife, Jennifer Brennan, and teaching at UVM fulfilled a lifelong dream.   Jim quickly achieved the rank of associate professor while also serving as interim director of the Consulting Archaeology Program.   In 2001, Jim was elected the Department Chair, a job he regularly admitted was not his favorite.   Even more than a promotion to full professor, Jim looked forward to next Spring ('06) when he would step down as Chair and be able to devote more time to his own research.

If only he had more time.   Jim was a top notch scholar who devoured the work of others with the same appetite he had for his own research.   His incredible capacity for work, his unequalled command of the literature and his own field work experience made him a leading authority in the archaeology of three major culture areas including northeastern North America, the Caribbean and lowland South America.   At the time of his death, he had active projects in four different countries with a long list of collaborators.

In addition to being an impeccable field scientist, Jim was a dedicated laboratory archaeologist devoted to the thorough study of human material culture.   His meticulous study of Amerindian technologies, including stone tools, ceramics, and perishable textiles, led to ground breaking insights into past social interaction and ethnicity.  

Jim was also thoroughly committed to publishing the results of his work.   His impressive record includes some seven monographs and edited volumes, 58 peer-reviewed articles and chapters, and hundreds of other articles, chapters and technical reports.   The volume of his published work is even more impressive considering his shortened career and how much of his precious free time he gave to others as a mentor, advisor, and colleague.

Over the last three decades, Jim analyzed hundreds of thousands of artifacts, all the time focused not on the objects themselves but what they could tell us about people.   He was, at the core, a people person.   This made him a fantastic anthropologist, as comfortable conversing with living people of all walks of life as he was using a shovel and trowel to uncover the past of others.

The University and the world have lost a gifted teacher, a legendary researcher, a great friend, and a generous and gentle man.
